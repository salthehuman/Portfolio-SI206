48 Hours in Paris

         We pulled in to Gare du Nord station around eleven in the morning, and as the train slowed down to anticipate its arrival, our hearts began to bounce as if they knew that this was Paris. Nineteen years old and on spring break from college, we rode into the romantic capital of the world holding hands, our clothing sharing one suitcase, our itinerary blank as the sky. You could read the story in our pupils, feel our energy, see our glow. We were ecstatic to be there, even if only for two nights.

         Sofia and I were staying in London for an entire week, so Paris was just meant to be an excursion. We traveled two hundred miles sitting comfortably next to a window, and counted delicate little churches as we darted through the countryside. We awed at our adventure and the excitement of switching cities, anxious to throw ourselves off guard and miscommunicate through a language barrier. In my backpack I brought journals by Hemingway and Kerouac, two American authors who followed artistic tradition to Paris. I longed to understand the magic of their muse, to breathe in the fine French air and smell the scents of their inspiration. Paris has a special reputation, earned for its elegance. I wanted to find out where it came from.

**

         Gare du Nord is a massive train terminal packed with activity, and on that Monday morning, its fast traffic was overwhelming. Men in suits were everywhere, rushing down walkways, late for their meetings, and young people in backpacks stayed barely a step behind. No one ever seemed to stop moving, not for a second, only us. We had no idea which way was up and it was written right on our faces. After following enough signs to finally find an entrance to the Metro, we stared blankly at the ticket machine with no idea what to purchase.

         I know we looked frazzled because immediately upon arriving we were greeted by a woman wearing a name tag. She was thin with black hair, tight pants and bright lipstick. Her eyes were embracive. They tried to lure us in. She claimed to be selling tickets and urged us that she was legitimate, but her smile had an attitude I immediately understood. When she asked how long we were staying, I knew that I shouldn’t answer, but something about denying her offer felt dangerous. She was persistent, and before I had a chance to send her off, a gray-haired man in a neon orange vest yelled: “She’s no good!”

Eventually she fled and we made our way to the train tunnels, but even underground, the atmosphere was equally threatening. Though most of the faces surrounding us seemed indifferent, the advertisements on the walls were unanimously ripped to shreds. Homeless people inhabited the foot of every staircase, and upon seeing one, I realized I was incapable of lying about money in French. Plus, an announcement on the loudspeaker kept warning to watch for pick-pockets, and though it spoke in the native tongue first, only repeating itself in English, I could not shake the thought that it was talking straight to me.

Sofia loves fine art and is a fully-trained ballerina. She is the type that should love Paris. The thought of accompanying her there felt noble. But standing on that dim-lit platform, guarding our suitcase and carrying our money, I realized having more responsibility made me vulnerable. I could not stop thinking about that woman selling tickets. The memory of her smile made me shiver.

**

            Our hotel was in the Opera District, right along Grands Boulevard, and immediately after checking in, we set out to see the city. The hotel’s concierge was a young, gregarious man who wore a tie clip, and though he often paused to think, he spoke almost perfect English. He wrote all over a map and gave us a lengthy list of sites, sending us out into the foreign streets with a well-established direction. The only problem was that neither one of us could follow it. We walked a mile to the left, having naturally been instructed to do the opposite, and by the time we realized our mistake, it seemed more fun to just keep going. So with the sky as bright as day, we walked aimlessly through the crowded streets, struggling to hide our naivety and laughing like two children, finally free from our parents’ watch.

Grands Boulevard is one of the largest, most active streets in Paris, and that morning we walked on to it wildly unprepared. Its six lanes of traffic were flooded with weekday commuters, its sidewalks hyper-active, its volume at a maximum. The buildings, all six stories tall with even wider wingspans, housed department stores with gigantic windows and recognizable restaurants. Uncreative graffiti tags violated every blank bit of wall, and right on the corner, a massive movie palace played American titles. As we walked, we shared obvious observations to re-assert our togetherness, counting the corporate storefronts and pointing out chairs that we called ‘French-looking’. We were overwhelmed by the atmosphere and unsure what to make of it. The humming sounds of rushing mopeds never ceased.

            When we finally did find an independent little creperie, answering our expectations with its purely native intentions, it was tucked away in a tiny slot, separated by a six lane highway, and of course, standing next to a Starbucks. I felt cheated by a city stained with recognizable evils and immediately imagined myself back home in New York. When Jack Kerouac wrote “Paris est pourri (Paris is rotten)”, that avenue must be what he was talking about.

**

            Then, later in that same walk, my attitude started to change. After taking another series of unjustified turns, Sofia and I found ourselves isolated on a narrow side street. Its buildings were thinner, their storefronts more specialized, and the road was so solemn that the sidewalk seemed unnecessary. We strolled slowly, soaking in the serenity of our discovered secret, and felt our lips quiver at the lingering smells of sandwich shops. We were starving, having skipped breakfast to catch our early train, but we were enthralled by our new whereabouts and unwilling to pause. We liked this neighborhood and wanted to understand it entirely, so we pushed forward, believing that one more block would make the difference.

            Somehow our subconscious must’ve been steering us all along, because at the end of that exact block, we stumbled upon a hidden treasure. There, tucked away from tourists, residing between apartment houses and bread shops, stood a colossal, old church with sandy stone walls and a papal projection.

It hovered atop a hill, dawning downward with elder authority, and a grand, stone staircase invited neighbors to its door. Its entrance was guarded by statues of famous saints, and to support their weight, six stone columns towered upward. At its sides, symmetric clock towers set unreachable peaks, and at the base, two curved paths circled around the stairs. We stood at its foot, gasping at the purity of its entrance into our lives, and wondered why the concierge hadn’t marked this on our map.

            That church turned out to be the Église Saint-Vincent-de-Paul and my memory of it is especially biased. After climbing up its path and earning a seat along the thick, rock railing, I looked out at the horizon and found a new nationalist urge. To my right the narrow road of Rue Fénelon was tightly squeezed, and its townhouses stacked six stories high in beautiful beige brick. Peeping through one of the windows, a gray-haired woman watched over her street, and beneath her, a neighbor’s French flag was flailing wildly. In that moment, I felt surrounded by a thousand unwritten stories. I realized that Paris is vast and I had much to explore.

**

            The first croissant I ate was like a cloud from the gates of heaven that bathed itself in butter and then baked. Every bite birthed new pleasures, every chew offered more comfort, and every swallow made me wish I’d stop sinning. Sitting in a quiet café, immersed in a late afternoon atmosphere, Sofia and I stuck out in the most American ways imaginable. First we pointed to place our order, incapable of pronouncing words, then we paused, dumbfounded, to decipher coins’ values. The cashier helped carry us, but he could only do so much. We sat down and waited for our drinks.

            The best cafés are not the big bullies haunting Grands Boulevard. I know this because being nineteen comes with a budget. Behind them though, hidden along some side street like a better kept secret, there’s bound to be a bakery within a couple blocks. Its space will be smaller and the traffic much slower, but the environment will be warmer and more welcoming to wanderers. Sofia and I ate most meals at a café of this sort. There was porridge for breakfast, pastries for lunch and bread, always bread. Even when we skipped dinner to save room for extra cocktails, the bartender brought a basket of warm, fresh baked bread.

            There is an art to living cheaply and we perfected the craft, eating more éclairs in two days than we did meals in a week. It wasn’t even money. We truly enjoyed doing it. Frugality helped us find the right places.

**

         Later that day, right as the sun was beginning to sink, Sofia and I climbed to the top of the Eiffel Tower. We walked up hundreds of stairs hunting for an evening orange sky, and though we’d expected to take the elevator, we wouldn’t let its query disrupt our timing. We were there for the sunset; seeing the stars was only secondary. So standing at the base of the world’s most famous bird cage, surrounded by flocks of faces from countries far and wide, we joined the bravest batch of tourists and started migrating toward the tip. Staircase tickets had the shortest line.

         The climb was steep, the concrete stairs cold, and rough winds roared louder with each step. The whole thing was confined by thick wiry walls, the kind of gated boundaries that tend to feel suffocating, but as we walked, the rare views growing visible kept us fueled. I began to anticipate feeling quite accomplished at the peak, and though my thighs started to ache, the physical sacrifice seemed pure. Our ears popped progressively rather than all at once and the largest portrait of Paris painted itself for us stroke by stroke. We stopped after each flight, gazed out through the wires’ weavings, and grew giddy like the kind of couple that might even mail home a postcard.

         When we finally reached the terrace, we felt ourselves consumed by clouds. The air was thin and the elevation apparent. Looking outward, an endless sea of rust-colored rooftops expanded, exposing entire neighborhoods and the whole essence of France. Skyscrapers across the river hinted at blossoming modernity, but closer to us, classic architecture cloaked the city. We were omnipotent, hovering above Notre Dame and the Arch de Triomphe, challenged only by the stars that would soon arrive to greet us. Of course there were others, so many others there to distract us, but the beauty of the moment outweighed all of their noise.

         The top floor of the Eiffel Tower has connotations that matter. Standing there in the flesh, I did not believe I was standing there. The soft touch of Sofia’s lips felt fake against my cheek.

**

         Mornings in Paris are the most tender in the world. When we awoke and opened the curtains, fictional beauty became tangible. Grands Boulevard is significantly less obnoxious when you’re expecting it, and by day two, I was so enthralled with the scene that nothing mattered. We rose without wasting a moment, our time in the city quickly dwindling, and raced out of the door to discover a cloudy sky. After eating breakfast at a nearby spot (another café on one of those side streets), we rushed to the Metro and boarded a train. The streets were cold and gray. Today was a day to stay inside.

            The Musée du Louvre is not for me and I will not glorify it here. Fondation Louis Vuitton though, a subtler gallery tucked toward the suburbs, is a striking structure to look at and a much more intimae experience. The glass castle, a colossal combination of curves and circles, sincerely looks like it should not be capable of standing. It’s white inside and out with natural light seeping in from all angles, and the art of its displays was overwhelmingly embracive. We walked into dark rooms, welcomed by movie screens or other artifacts, and were physically pulled into every artist’s distinct frame. The work was all Chinese and a lot of it called upon evils of Communism, but one piece was a quirky animation about urban sprawl. We stayed there for four hours, and I can assure you it’s not very big. Something about its oddity excited us.

When we did leave, the sun was hiding and the evening soon arriving. We returned to our hotel to take showers and change.

**

               That night we were awake as only those without time can be. It was our last night in Paris and we wanted to run wild. We took the Metro down to Oberkampf to explore untested side streets, and after comparing twelve different menus, we sat at a wine bar serving small plates. We immediately ordered cocktails, one of which came with its top lit aflame, and fifteen minutes later we were sharing calmars frits. The restaurant was lit by candles and we were the youngest people there. The waiter spoke perfect English. He had tattoos and wore jeans. Most of the crowd was late twenty-something and sipping slowly. Sofia and I shared salmon. Most of our money went to drinks. 
               After dinner we crossed the street for a few nightcaps at a bar called ‘The Kitch’. It was a tiny, colorful place disastrously decorated in vintage. There were just two tables to sit at and everything was carefully disorganized. Bulky radios with long-antennas sat atop every shelf. The bartenders code-switched by customer between English and French, and when Sofia ordered a Caipirinha, the tanner of the two asked if she was Brazilian. They shared a moment, mutually excited speak to someone in Portugese, and two drinks later, parted ways with a sincere “Ciao!” We set out beneath the midnight sky with no idea which way was home. We wanted to get lost in the city.
               Just a few blocks from Rue de Oberkampf there is a massive plaza fit for thousands. At its heart, a seventy-foot statue stands atop a towering pedestal. The bronze goddess reaches her right arm out toward the the city, strengthening the streets even through their current sleep. At her base, flowers and posters were proudly celebrating freedom. I saw the words “Je Suis Charlie” and realized it was a tribute against terrorism. I analyzed the handwritten nationalist phrases and the streaky spots where blue marker mixed with lines of red. I imagined young children dreaming up their proud creations and felt myself a Frenchmen, honored to side with their values. 
               America wouldn’t exist without France’s revolutionary spirit. The air in the Place de la République offered powerful reminders of this.
**

         Those new bicycle sharing system, with their corporate sponsors and fake, plastic tires, are popping up in every big city across the world. Paris is not an exception. So having agreed to roam the streets wildly throughout our final moments, Sofia and I rented two and took off towards the river. We rode along its side, following green lights wherever they guided us, and savored the morning silence. The entire scene was soothing. Cars crept rather than racing and the roads’ rhythm was calm. Even when it started to rain, the clouds could only drizzle. We peddled forward, Sofia’s wet hair waving in the wind, without a single care for the outside world. Paris was the only place to be.

         We peddled past Place de la Concorde, finally appreciating its proudly lit Ferris Wheel, then rushed down Avenue des Champs-Élysée to find more early morning serenity. The long avenue, generally defined by its busy traffic and bright advertisements, was abandoned by its daytime visitors and dedicated to silence. Every store was closed. The only motion was our racing bikes. We rode down to the Arch de Triomphe, then finally paused to appreciate stillness. I’m usually not syrupy. In fact, I’m quite sarcastic. But sharing a kiss amidst a Parisian rainstorm is the pinnacle of romance.

         The next time we stopped moving it was to watch the Eiffel Tower. At night, before signing off, its lights blink rapidly to wave goodbye. They sparkled beneath the stars, bouncing across the sky like lightning, and standing there I thought that I could never write this down. Everything was too perfect. No one would ever believe it. 

**
               The next day we boarded a train to take our bodies back to London, and as we rode, every mile made my heart sink an inch lower. I went to Paris to eat pastries and post their photos on Instagram, to seem a cultured wanderlust while my friends did drugs in Mexico. What I found there though was more complex than a vacation spent abroad. A tiny piece of my soul now eternally lives in France. Traveling to Paris is an investment in the future. It’s like reading great literature – things don’t click on the first glance. But if you’re eager and excited enough to look back in search of meaning, then eventually you’ll start to see solutions to its puzzle. 
               Paris is a city, a massive, international metropolis, with borders that stretch far beyond its reputation. Its sites are not its summary; words will not write its proper portrait. It cannot be understood until its walked through and reflected on.
